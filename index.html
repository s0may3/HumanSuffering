Afghan Migration: Persistent Pressure and Uncertain Futures

Designed by: Somayyeh Eslami

Overview: migration pressure over time

This project explores Afghan migration not as a single event, but as a multi-stage human process. Over time, outward migration from Afghanistan follows a recognisable pattern. Long stretches of relative stability are visible, followed by sudden surges that sharply break the line. These jumps do not fade away. After each surge, migration settles at a higher level than before, and pressure accumulates rather than returning to earlier lows. This rising baseline forms the starting point of the story. Periods of economic stress appear when price changes become volatile and uneven, revealing instability rather than smooth trends. Pressure first concentrates inside the country, where internal displacement clusters in specific regions. From there, movement follows established cross-border corridors, not random dispersion. Arrival does not resolve displacement. A small number of destination countries absorb a disproportionate share of people, often for multiple years. Across time, outcomes remain dominated by prolonged refugee and asylum statuses, while returns appear episodic rather than sustained. Together, these patterns reveal migration as an ongoing condition shaped by persistence, inequality, and uncertainty at every stage.

Shocks and economic volatility

Instead of looking at migration numbers alone, it is important to consider the economic conditions that shape everyday decisions. Changes in food prices reveal periods of instability that households experience directly. When year-to-year price changes become wider and more uneven, they reflect moments of economic stress rather than gradual adjustment. These periods are marked not only by rising prices, but by increased uncertainty and extreme variations. Such volatility affects livelihoods and coping strategies, shaping the broader context in which mobility and displacement occur. Looking beyond averages and examining the full range of price changes makes this uncertainty visible, highlighting how economic shocks translate into lived pressure over time.This pattern suggests that economic pressure is driven less by steady price increases and more by volatility and unpredictability, which directly shape household vulnerability.

Inside Afghanistan: internal displacement (IDPs)

After examining long-term migration pressure and economic volatility, we shift the focus inside Afghanistan. Not all displacement immediately crosses international borders. Internal displacement is often the first visible stage of migration pressure, reflecting how stress accumulates unevenly across space. This map shows the distribution of internally displaced people (IDPs) across provinces. Rather than explaining causes, it highlights spatial concentration — where displacement burdens are highest at a given point in time.

Cross-border destinations and corridors (latest year)

Up to this point, the story has focused on pressure and displacement within Afghanistan. The next step is to look beyond the border. When we examine cross-border movements, a clear pattern emerges: migration does not disperse evenly across destinations. Instead, movements concentrate along a limited set of corridors and toward a small number of destination countries. Visually, this concentration is evident in both the direction and thickness of flows. Some routes stand out clearly, while many other destinations receive comparatively smaller shares. This suggests that cross-border migration follows established pathways rather than random dispersion. What this view makes visible is not just where people go, but how unevenly movement is distributed across routes and destinations. The result is a highly asymmetric pattern of cross-border migration, in which a few corridors and countries absorb a disproportionate share of outward movement. This view represents a snapshot of the latest available year, setting the stage for the next question: how this uneven movement translates into hosting pressure and longer-term outcomes.

Beyond routes: hosting pressure

The previous view showed how Afghan displacement moves across borders. Here, the focus shifts from movement to consequence. Once people arrive, displacement does not end. It turns into hosting pressure — the uneven and often persistent responsibility of absorbing displaced populations. What stands out in this comparison is concentration. A small number of destination countries host disproportionately large shares of Afghan refugees, and in several cases these populations persist or grow over time. The key insight is not where people go, but how unevenly and durably the burden of displacement is distributed across destinations.

Protection, return, and unresolved outcomes

Migration does not end at arrival. This final view examines what happens after displacement, by looking at how outcomes are distributed over time between return, refugee status, and asylum-seeking. Rather than focusing on absolute numbers, the chart shows the composition of outcomes, making it possible to see which statuses dominate and how this balance shifts across years. What stands out is persistence: refugee and asylum statuses remain the majority for long periods, while returns appear episodic rather than sustained. This pattern suggests that displacement often remains unresolved even after crossing borders. Taken together, the project shows migration not as a single movement, but as a prolonged process shaped by pressure, inequality, and uncertainty at every stage.

Data processing and analysis

The analytical pipeline in this project follows a clear and reproducible sequence, transforming raw humanitarian data into interpretable metrics and visual encodings. The goal is not prediction, but the identification of patterns and processes.

  • Raw data ingestion: CSV and GeoJSON files are loaded directly from the dataset/ directory using D3.js. These files originate from HDX HAPI and related humanitarian sources.
  • Cleaning and normalization: Raw values are converted into numeric formats, reporting periods are normalized to annual values, and invalid or non-informative rows are excluded (e.g. missing year, non-positive counts, or inconsistent location codes).
  • Aggregation and metrics: Cleaned records are aggregated into metrics aligned with the narrative: annual outflow totals, latest-year destination rankings, province-level IDP counts, and year-to-year food price changes.
  • Mapping to visual encodings: Each metric is mapped to a specific visual form, including time-series lines for long-term pressure, distribution-based density plots for economic shocks, choropleth maps for spatial concentration, and flow diagrams for cross-border movement.
  • Narrative integration: Metrics are not shown in isolation. They are sequenced into a six-phase narrative (pressure, displacement, routes, destinations, uneven hosting, and unknown paths), guiding the user from contextual evidence to interpretive insight.

Limitations and uncertainty

  • Reporting gaps: Humanitarian datasets are subject to reporting delays and uneven coverage across regions and years. Some movements may not be captured in official records.
  • Aggregation bias: Several indicators are aggregated at national or annual levels. This limits fine-grained temporal or local interpretation.
  • Definition variability: Categories such as refugees, asylum-seekers, and returnees may follow different definitions depending on the reporting source and update cycle.
  • Unknown destinations: Records without a clear destination are treated explicitly as uncertainty. “Unknown” does not indicate absence of movement, but limitations of data visibility.
