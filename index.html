Designed by: Somayyeh Eslami

Overview: migration pressure over time

We begin by looking at the long-term evolution of outward migration from Afghanistan.

Rather than focusing on individual events, this overview highlights how migration pressure has persisted and changed over time. Viewing the full timeline helps establish a baseline before examining specific shocks, routes, and destinations in later sections.

Units: people (counts). Time scale: annual. Geography: origin Afghanistan → destination country.

Shocks and economic volatility

Instead of looking at migration directly, this section focuses on economic instability as contextual background.

The chart shows the distribution of food price changes over time. Large deviations in this distribution represent periods of economic shock, highlighting how volatility — rather than gradual trends — shapes everyday conditions.

Chart type: histogram of food price changes. Values represent year-to-year changes, aggregated across commodities.

Inside Afghanistan: internal displacement (IDPs)

After examining long-term migration pressure and economic volatility, we shift the focus inside Afghanistan. Not all displacement immediately crosses international borders. Internal displacement is often the first visible stage of migration pressure, reflecting how stress accumulates unevenly across space. This map shows the distribution of internally displaced people (IDPs) across provinces. Rather than explaining causes, it highlights spatial concentration — where displacement burdens are highest at a given point in time.

Map type: Choropleth (admin1). Units: people (counts).

Cross-border destinations and corridors (latest year)

Up to this point, we examined pressure and displacement inside Afghanistan. Now we look beyond the border.

This section maps the most common destination countries for Afghan refugees and asylum-seekers in the latest available year. The visualization highlights both concentration (which countries receive the most) and direction (corridors from Afghanistan to destinations).

Map + flow arrows + Sankey. Data: refugee and asylum-seeker counts aggregated by destination country (latest year).

Beyond routes: hosting pressure

The previous section illustrated the main cross-border routes taken by Afghan refugees and the relative scale of these movements. However, the significance of these flows cannot be fully understood by volume alone.

Once refugees arrive at their destinations, the question shifts from where people go to how hosting countries absorb and sustain these populations. Different destinations face markedly different levels of pressure, depending not only on the number of arrivals, but also on their demographic and economic capacity.

This section therefore compares Afghan refugee destinations in relation to host-country capacity, highlighting how unevenly the burden of displacement is distributed across destination countries.

Protection, return, and unresolved outcomes

Migration does not end at arrival. For many displaced Afghans, destination countries can represent a temporary stage rather than a final resolution.

This final section shifts the focus from movement and destinations to outcomes: protection status, return-related pressure, and the constraints that shape long-term stability. It frames displacement as an ongoing condition that can remain unresolved even after crossing borders.

Conceptual focus: protection, return, and uncertainty in displacement outcomes.

Methodology

This website presents Afghan migration as a multi-stage human process, using curated humanitarian datasets and interactive visualizations.

Data processing and analysis

The analytical pipeline in this project follows a clear and reproducible sequence, transforming raw humanitarian data into interpretable metrics and visual encodings. The goal is not prediction, but the identification of patterns and processes.

  • Raw data ingestion: CSV and GeoJSON files are loaded directly from the dataset/ directory using D3.js. These files originate from HDX HAPI and related humanitarian sources.
  • Cleaning and normalization: Raw values are converted into numeric formats, reporting periods are normalized to annual values, and invalid or non-informative rows are excluded (e.g. missing year, non-positive counts, or inconsistent location codes).
  • Aggregation and metrics: Cleaned records are aggregated into metrics aligned with the narrative: annual outflow totals, latest-year destination rankings, province-level IDP counts, and year-to-year food price changes.
  • Mapping to visual encodings: Each metric is mapped to a specific visual form: time-series lines for long-term pressure, histograms for economic shocks, choropleth maps for spatial concentration, and flow diagrams for cross-border movement.
  • Narrative integration: Metrics are not shown in isolation. They are sequenced into a six-phase narrative (pressure, displacement, routes, destinations, uneven hosting, and unknown paths), guiding the user from contextual evidence to interpretive insight.

Limitations and uncertainty

  • Reporting gaps: Humanitarian datasets are subject to reporting delays and uneven coverage across regions and years. Some movements may not be captured in official records.
  • Aggregation bias: Several indicators are aggregated at national or annual levels. This limits fine-grained temporal or local interpretation.
  • Definition variability: Categories such as refugees, asylum-seekers, and returnees may follow different definitions depending on the reporting source and update cycle.
  • Unknown destinations: Records without a clear destination are treated explicitly as uncertainty. “Unknown” does not indicate absence of movement, but limitations of data visibility.
